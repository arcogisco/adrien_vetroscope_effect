# adrien_vetroscope_effect
A website mock Adrien's atomsphere under vetroscope.

## Controls
- **Mouse wheel:** Zoom
- **Drag up/down:** Tilt
- **Drag left/right:** Rotate around the Y-axis
- **Hand gestures:** Control zoom and tilt simultaneously

## Notes
- On first launch, please allow the browser to access your camera.
- This page depends on online CDN resources: `three.js` and `MediaPipe Hands`.
- An internet connection is required for the full experience to load.

## About Adrian
Adrian is a pale green to vivid green world wrapped in vast swirling cloud bands, with scattered orange-red and rust-colored patches across its atmosphere. From space, it looks like a luminous green planet, almost like a fluorescent gas giant.

In *Project Hail Mary*, Adrian is a **rocky super-Earth** with a surface gravity of about **1.4× Earth’s** and a low-orbit velocity of roughly **12 km/s**. Its radius is estimated to be around **1.6× Earth’s**, likely somewhere in the **1.5–2× Earth** range. Although it is a rocky planet, its extremely thick atmosphere makes it look more like a giant banded world from afar.

Its atmosphere is composed of about **91% carbon dioxide**, **7% methane**, with small amounts of argon. This dense atmosphere churns into enormous storm bands and rolling cloud systems, creating a Jupiter-like appearance. The activity of **Astrophage** further intensifies this effect, making the planet glow vividly green in visible light.

The green color comes from the interaction between **Astrophage** and methane, which scatter and emit light mainly in the green part of the spectrum. The orange-red patches likely mark areas of higher Astrophage concentration or localized chemical activity within the clouds, resembling storm markings like Jupiter’s Great Red Spot.

When viewed through the **Petrovascope** in infrared mode, denser concentrations of Astrophage appear as striking **red cloud bands**, revealing hidden structures inside Adrian’s glowing atmosphere.
